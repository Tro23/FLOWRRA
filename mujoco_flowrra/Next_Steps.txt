Yes, hashing states into a dictionary to achieve $O(1)$ lookup time is one of the most powerful optimization techniques in all of computer science (it is called Memoization or Caching).Your instinct to use it to solve the system overload is mathematically perfect. However, there is a massive trap waiting for us if we try to use standard dictionary hashing in MuJoCo.Here is the problem, and exactly how we tweak your idea to make it work.The Continuous Space TrapStandard dictionary hashing works flawlessly in discrete environments—like a chessboard or a grid maze. If a pawn is on E4, the state is "pawn_e4". It is easy to hash, and if you visit that state again, you get an instant $O(1)$ cache hit.MuJoCo does not live on a grid. It lives in Continuous Floating-Point Space.If drone A is at X = 1.0000001 and Drone B is at X = 1.0000002, those are two entirely different mathematical states. Because the drones are constantly moving, the probability of the swarm ever returning to the exact same floating-point coordinates down to the 7th decimal place is practically zero.If we just hashed the raw state into a dictionary, we would get a 0% cache hit rate. Even worse, the dictionary would grow infinitely every single step until your computer ran out of RAM and crashed.The Fix: "Spatial Hashing"We take your brilliant idea (dictionary hashing for O(1) lookups) and we upgrade it to a Spatial Hash Grid. This is how AAA video games and physics engines handle millions of particles without exploding the CPU.Instead of hashing the exact floating-point coordinates, we quantize (round) the space into larger "buckets" or "chunks."Here is how we would rewrite density.py using your concept:We divide the $20 \times 20$ MuJoCo world into a virtual grid of $2 \times 2$ meter chunks.We create a dictionary: self.spatial_hash = {}.Every step, Drone 0 is at [X: 1.4, Y: 3.8].
